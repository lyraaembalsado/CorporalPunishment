PAUSE
Positive Alternatives to Unsafe & Severe Enforcement
What will you do in the next 5 seconds?
This flipbook walks you through a simple 5-step framework for responding to children with care — not fear.

Use it alone, in a workshop, or with a group. Keep it close for the moments that matter.
10 pages · Pilot edition · Asia-Pacific
Before we start
"Think about how you were disciplined as a child."
What happened when you made a mistake? How did it feel? Do you still carry that feeling today?
Most of us were never taught another way. PAUSE gives you one.
As you read each step, think of a real moment.
A time your child made a mistake. A time you reacted in a way you wish you hadn't. That moment is where PAUSE begins.
Step 1 of 5
P
Pause
Stop before you react. Take one breath. Give yourself 5 seconds before you do or say anything.
The urge to react comes from the body, not the mind. One breath creates enough space to choose what happens next.
In the moment
Your child broke something after you told them not to. You feel the anger rise. Before anything else — breathe. Just breathe.
Step 2 of 5
A
Ask
Ask the child what happened. Let them speak first. Listen without interrupting.
Children often act out for reasons adults don't see. Asking before assuming shows respect — and gets you closer to the truth.
In the moment
"What happened?" — said calmly, not as an accusation. Wait for the answer even if it takes a moment to come.
Step 3 of 5
U
Understand
Consider why they did it. Were they scared? Confused? Bored? Testing limits? Still learning?
Behavior is communication. What looks like defiance is often a need the child doesn't know how to express yet.
In the moment
Ask yourself: "What was going on for them?" Not to excuse the behavior — but to understand it before you respond.
Step 4 of 5
S
Support
Respond to the real need behind the behavior — not just the behavior itself. Guide, don't punish.
Children learn better through guidance than fear. A child who is supported feels safe enough to do better next time.
In the moment
What does your child actually need right now? Calm redirection. A clear boundary, explained. Your presence, not your anger.
Step 5 of 5
E
Encourage
End with something that builds trust, not fear. Let them know the mistake doesn't change how much you love them.
Children who feel loved after a mistake are more likely to be honest, try again, and trust the adults in their lives.
In the moment
"I know you can do better. I'm here." That's it. That's enough.
Your commitment
Is that the memory you want your child to have of you?
You don't have to be perfect. You just have to pause — one moment at a time.

For facilitators
How to run a PAUSE session
Before the session
Print one flipbook per participant, or share this link on a shared screen. Prepare blank paper for offline reflections if needed.
What to collect for your pilot report
After the session, gather the commitment pages. You are looking for:

· Total participants who completed the flipbook
· % who named a support person
· % who wrote a specific commit-to moment
· Notable quotes from "one thing I learned"
· Your own observation: how did the group respond?
Follow-up (3–4 weeks later)
Ask one question: "Did you use PAUSE at least once since our session?" Yes / No / Not yet but I tried. That single data point across 10+ participants is your behavior change evidence.
Bring this to the table
Printed commitment pages + follow-up responses = the proof that PAUSE works in real communities. This is what you bring to the 2nd Global Ministerial Conference in November 2026.

Safeguarding commitment
Everyone here is safe.
This commitment applies to all PAUSE facilitators, team members, and partners — in every session, in every country.
For children
No child will ever be hit, shamed, or humiliated during PAUSE activities.
Children will never be photographed or identified without informed consent.
Any disclosure of abuse will be reported immediately to the Focal Person and referred to the appropriate authority.
For adult participants
Sharing is always voluntary. No one will be pressured to disclose personal experiences.
If a participant becomes distressed, the facilitator will pause the session and offer private support.
All responses and personal information shared are kept confidential within the team.
